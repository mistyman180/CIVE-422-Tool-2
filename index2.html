Tool Description

What it does. This tool compares two engineering alternatives for reducing respirable dust (PM₁₀) emissions from haul road traffic at a Front Range aggregate quarry, where the critical receptor is an elementary school approximately 400 m downwind. The alternatives are periodic water truck application and magnesium chloride (MgCl₂) chemical suppressant.

Inputs. The tool accepts user-adjustable inputs for operational parameters (vehicle silt content, mean vehicle weight, trips per hour, haul road length), meteorology (wind speed, Pasquill-Gifford atmospheric stability class, receptor distance), and control-specific parameters (water truck application interval, MgCl₂ application age).

Modeling approach. The tool uses a simplified Gaussian line-source dispersion model with Pasquill-Gifford sigma coefficients (Martin 1976 power-law formulation) to estimate ground-level PM₁₀ concentrations at the receptor. Uncontrolled emission factors are computed from the AP-42 §13.2.2 unpaved road equation, and each control alternative is applied as a time-weighted or age-dependent control efficiency. A line source is appropriate here because the haul road is long relative to the receptor distance and emissions are distributed along its length.

Outputs. The tool produces (1) 24-hour average PM₁₀ concentrations at the receptor for both alternatives, compared to the 150 µg/m³ NAAQS; (2) concentration-vs-distance plots; (3) sensitivity plots for wind speed and stability class; (4) a four-criterion MCDA matrix; and (5) an AI-generated interpretation of the results.

Interpretation. A user should compare the two concentration metrics against the NAAQS and against each other, then inspect the MCDA matrix to weigh tradeoffs in cost, health impact, and environmental burden. The sensitivity tabs reveal conditions under which each alternative fails — low wind speeds and stable atmospheres make all interventions less adequate and favor the more aggressive control.
